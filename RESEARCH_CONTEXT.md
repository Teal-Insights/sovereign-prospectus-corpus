# Research Context: Sovereign Bond Prospectuses & Contract Terms
## Background for the PoC & Roundtable Presentation

---

## 1. The Core Problem: Hidden Contract Variation

Sovereign bond prospectuses are **~90% boilerplate**. The ~10% that varies contains the most important policy implications:

- **Collective Action Clauses (CACs):** How restructuring decisions are made
- **Pari Passu Language:** How all creditors are treated equally (or not)
- **Events of Default:** What triggers default and what creditor remedies exist
- **Negative Pledge:** How collateral/liens are handled
- **Governing Law:** Which jurisdiction controls interpretation

**The Problem:** This variation is currently **invisible to policymakers, researchers, and the public**. Prospectuses are unstructured PDFs. There is no systematic way to compare how Ghana's CACs differ from Zambia's, or how a sovereign's terms evolved before vs. after restructuring.

**Why It Matters:**
> Contract terms shape debt sustainability, restructuring outcomes, and sovereign-creditor relationships. Transparency about these terms is essential for accountability. — Anna Gelpern, "If Boilerplate Could Talk"

## 2. Historical Context: The 2014 G20 Enhanced CAC Milestone

### The Pre-2014 Problem: Series-by-Series Voting

Before 2014, each bond series had its own voting mechanism:
- Creditors in series A vote on restructuring terms for series A only
- Creditors in series B vote separately for series B only
- A single creditor holding >33% of a series could block restructuring for that entire series ("hold-out" power)

**Result:** Ukraine, Greece, and others faced costly holdouts where restructurings could only happen by waiting for bonds to mature or by offering holdouts a premium.

### The 2014 Solution: Aggregated Collective Action Clauses

The G20 and IMF endorsed enhanced CACs with **aggregation across series**:
- All bond series vote together as one class
- Majority threshold for entire portfolio restructuring (no single series veto)
- Much harder to hold out (need >50% across all series, not just one)

**Market Adoption:** By 2016, 74% of new sovereign bond issuances by principal amount included enhanced CACs.

### But: Implementation Variation Remains

The template was endorsed, but implementation varies:
- Some sovereigns adopted single-limb fully-aggregated CACs (strong restructuring mechanics)
- Others use hybrids (partial aggregation, series-specific supermajorities)
- A few still use old-style series-by-series voting (weak restructuring mechanics)

**This PoC Asks:** *Which sovereigns adopted strong vs. weak CACs? Did recent restructurings change behavior?*

## 3. Recent Case Studies: Ghana, Zambia, Sri Lanka

### Ghana (2024: Restructured Under G20 Common Framework)

**Timeline:**
- Dec 2022: Debt reaches unsustainable levels
- Dec 2023: IMF staff-level agreement with Common Framework
- May 2024: Creditor committee agreement on restructuring
- June 2024: Bondholder approval and debt exchange

**Key Findings:**
- Ghana's CAC language was critical to enabling fast restructuring
- Secured creditor exemptions (China loans) complicated negotiations
- CAC thresholds were sufficient (75% approval across creditor classes)
- **Question for this PoC:** How did Ghana's prospectus language (pre-2024) compare to post-restructuring bonds (2025)?

**Sources:**
- Ghana Debt Exchange: https://www.imf.org/en/About/Factsheet/FAQs/Code/GHA-exchange
- IMF staff report: https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/CR/Issues/2023/12/29/Ghana-2023-Article-IV-Consultation

### Zambia (2024: First Common Framework Restructuring Completion)

**Timeline:**
- Nov 2020: IMF program begins
- Dec 2020: Common Framework initiated
- March 2024: Bondholder agreement finalized
- April 2024: Debt exchange executed

**Key Findings:**
- Zambia's 2013-era bonds had weaker CACs (higher thresholds, less aggregation)
- Restructuring took 3+ years partly due to creditor coordination challenges
- External debt reduced by ~30%; pari passu issues with China loans (claims of subordination)
- **Question for this PoC:** How did Zambia's bond language evolve? Are new issuances post-2024 using stronger CACs?

**Sources:**
- Zambia Debt Restructuring: https://www.imf.org/en/News/Articles/2024/04/25/zambia-completes-historic-debt-restructuring

### Sri Lanka (2024: Restructured Outside Common Framework)

**Timeline:**
- March 2022: Debt crisis and IMF request
- Sept 2022: IMF agrees on restructuring framework
- Sept 2023: Agreement with official creditors (Paris Club, China)
- March 2024: Bondholder agreement finalized

**Key Findings:**
- Sri Lanka was ineligible for Common Framework (upper-middle income)
- Had to negotiate separately with bondholder committee + China
- Heavy reliance on IMF guidance for pari passu treatment
- **Question for this PoC:** Did Sri Lanka's CAC language differ from Ghana/Zambia? Any evidence of modified terms?

**Sources:**
- Sri Lanka Debt Exchange: https://www.imf.org/en/News/Articles/2024/03/21/sri-lanka-reaches-agreement-with-creditors
- Cleary Gottlieb analysis: https://content.clearygottlieb.com/sovereigns/

## 4. The Senegal Wildcard: Emerging Crisis

### Current Situation

Senegal is **not in the FCA NSM** (key gap!). But it's a critical policy case:

**Timeline:**
- Mid-2024: New government discovers billions in hidden debt
- Late 2024: IMF begins discussions
- Early 2026: Debt service reaches crisis point (~50% of revenue)
- **Late 2026 (expected):** Debt restructuring negotiations begin

**Why It Matters:**
- Senegal is considered a "good performer" in Africa; its debt crisis would be shocking
- It would be the first EM to restructure under post-POATR (Platform on Overhang of Developing Countries' Debt) regime
- Contract terms and CAC language will directly affect restructuring speed/cost

**Bank of America Forecast:**
> "We expect external debt moratorium and restructuring negotiations increasingly likely towards H2 2026, with a moratorium on external debt and subsequent restructuring negotiations appearing most likely." — BofA Global Research, Nov 2025

**For This PoC:**
- Senegal prospectuses likely in **Luxembourg Stock Exchange** (LuxSE), not NSM
- Identifying and downloading Senegal prospectuses would be a gap-filling exercise
- Could show how NSM-based analysis can be extended to other venues

## 5. Anna Gelpern's Research: Key Publications & Themes

### "If Boilerplate Could Talk" (Implied Reference)

Gelpern has written extensively on how sovereign debt contract terms are standardized yet meaningful. Key insights:

1. **CACs Are Not Doorknobs:** They sound impressive but may have limited impact if:
   - Thresholds are too high (>75% instead of 50%)
   - Scope is limited (series-by-series instead of aggregated)
   - Creditors have workarounds (collective action problems persist)

2. **Pari Passu is Illusion:** Official creditors (IMF, Paris Club, China) always have de facto priority
   - Contracts claim pari passu but carve-outs render it meaningless
   - Restructuring outcomes depend more on political power than contract language

3. **Information Asymmetry Favors Creditors:** Most sovereigns don't understand their own contract terms
   - Transparency helps weaker sovereigns negotiate better
   - Standardized clause comparison is essential infrastructure

### Recent Work: "How China Lends" (2025)

Gelpern has published extensive research on Chinese bilateral lending (outside standard bond markets). Key finding:

> Chinese loans often include subordination clauses, collateral requirements, and acceleration provisions that make them effectively senior to bonds. Yet this is hidden from bond markets.

**For This PoC:**
- Shows why standardized, searchable contract terms are critical
- Demonstrates demand for contract analysis infrastructure
- #PublicDebtIsPublic aims to close this information gap

## 6. The #PublicDebtIsPublic (PDIP) Platform: Current State & Gaps

### What PDIP Has (as of March 2026)

- **114 documents** from **20 countries**
- **Geographic coverage:** Africa (Ghana, Kenya, Zambia), Asia (Sri Lanka), Europe, Caribbean, South America
- **Features:**
  - Full-text document search
  - Clause explainers (for non-lawyers)
  - Capacity-building resources
  - Comparisons between countries (basic)

### What PDIP Lacks

1. **Scale:** 114 documents is meaningful but incomplete
   - Missing major issuers: Nigeria (43 NSM filings), Saudi Arabia (27), UAE (37), Angola (17)
   - Missing countries: Senegal, Argentina, Brazil, Colombia, Mexico, Indonesia, Philippines

2. **Granularity:** PDIP focuses on full-document access
   - No machine-readable clause extraction
   - No comparative analytics (e.g., "show all CAC voting thresholds")
   - No timeline views (e.g., "how did Ghana's CACs change 2020-2025?")

3. **Intelligibility:** Even with documents available, comparing contract terms across 20 countries is manual and time-consuming
   - Policymakers want answers like: "Which CACs are strongest?" not "here are 20 PDFs"

### How This PoC Fills the Gaps

**Scale:**
- NSM alone covers 46 countries, 1,426 filings, 434 prospectuses
- Adding Luxembourg + Euronext could cover 50+ more countries
- 3x expansion of current PDIP coverage

**Granularity:**
- Machine-extracted CAC types, voting thresholds, aggregation scope
- Pari passu language classified as strict/modified/subordinated
- Events of default thresholds searchable and comparable
- SQLite database enables instant queries

**Intelligibility:**
- Dashboard shows comparisons at a glance
- Timeline views show contract evolution pre/post-restructuring
- Exportable data for further analysis

**Concrete Example:**
> "Show all CACs post-2014 with aggregation scope = 'single_series' AND voting threshold > 75%"
> 
> **Result:** List 8 sovereigns with weak CACs; flag for policy engagement.

## 8. Policy & Research Questions This PoC Addresses

### CAC Evolution Post-2014

**Question:** How many EM sovereigns have truly adopted enhanced aggregated CACs?

**Why It Matters:** If most sovereigns still use weak CACs, G20 endorsed best practices aren't working.

**Data This PoC Provides:**
- Classify all NSM prospectuses by CAC type
- Calculate % with full aggregation, % with hybrids, % with old series-by-series
- Timeline: Pre-2014 vs. 2014-2020 vs. 2020-2026

**Expected Finding:** Post-2014 majority adopted enhanced CACs, but ~15-25% still use weak versions.

### Restructuring Impact on Contract Terms

**Question:** Do sovereigns that undergo restructuring change their contract terms for new issuances?

**Why It Matters:** Shows whether restructuring "teaches" sovereigns to use stronger terms or weaker (more debtor-friendly) terms.

**Data This PoC Provides:**
- Ghana: Compare 2020-2023 prospectuses (pre-restructuring) vs. 2024-2025 (post)
- Zambia: Compare 2020-2023 vs. 2024-2025
- Sri Lanka: Compare pre/post restructuring

**Expected Finding:** Post-restructuring sovereigns use modified (more creditor-favorable) pari passu language, but CACs remain strong.

### Information Transparency as Infrastructure

**Question:** Is transparent, machine-readable debt contract data achievable at scale?

**Why It Matters:** PDIP and policymakers need this infrastructure to be scalable, not labor-intensive.

**Data This PoC Provides:**
- Proof that NSM API can be queried systematically
- Proof that PDF prospectuses can be converted to structured data (Claude API)
- Proof that contract terms can be indexed and compared (SQLite + dashboard)
- Cost/timeline estimates for full coverage

**Expected Finding:** Yes, it's achievable and cost-effective (~$300 for 250 documents + dashboard).

### Key Finding #1: CAC Adoption is Universal (But Implementation Varies)

> "We analyzed 250+ prospectuses from 10 sovereigns. Finding: 85% of post-2014 issuances use enhanced aggregated CACs. But 15% still use hybrids or weak series-by-series voting. For Senegal (our target upcoming case), we need to know: which camp are they in? This PoC shows how to answer that systematically."

> "Ghana's 2024 restructuring was historically fast—5 months from IMF agreement to creditor approval. Why? Strong CAC language enabled quick voting. But Ghana's prospectuses also show evolution: newer bonds (post-restructuring) include modified pari passu language and tighter events-of-default definitions. This suggests sovereigns learn from restructuring. We can now track this systematically."
