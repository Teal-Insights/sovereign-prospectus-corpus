## Executive Summary

Senegal is a high-priority target for the Sovereign Bond Prospectus Corpus project due to its emerging debt distress risk and transparent market documentation. While Senegal is NOT listed in the FCA National Storage Mechanism (NSM), it has issued multiple Eurobonds listed on **Euronext Dublin** and **Luxembourg Stock Exchange (LuxSE)**, with prospectuses accessible through both venues. This research identifies specific download locations, West African market patterns, and recommendations for corpus expansion.

## 1. SENEGAL'S DEBT SITUATION AND POLICY RELEVANCE

### 1.1 The Crisis Context

Senegal has emerged as a textbook case of unexpected sovereign debt distress, making it highly relevant for debt restructuring research:

- **Debt-to-GDP Ratio:** 132% at end-2024 (IMF estimate), with total outstanding Eurobonds approximately USD 1.1 billion
- **Hidden Debt Scandal:** March 2025 Court of Auditors report revealed USD 13 billion in undeclared debt (equivalent to 25% of GDP, 2019-2024), the largest hidden debt in any IMF program country
- **Political Context:** New government under President Bassirou Diomaye Faye (elected April 2024) blamed predecessor Macky Sall; inherited debt crisis coincided with promised "sovereignty restoration"
- **Restructuring Resistance:** Prime Minister Ousmane Sonko publicly rejected IMF restructuring proposals as a "disgrace," framing austerity measures as political poison post-election

### 1.2 Immediate Financial Stress

- **March 2026 Payments Due:** Senegal faced approximately USD 485 million in Eurobond repayments (including €333.3 million principal on 2028 bonds)
- **Bond Trading Distress:** Multiple Eurobonds trading at 50-70 cents on the dollar; yield demands of 20% on some instruments indicate near-default probability
- **Recent Developments (Feb-March 2026):** Government successfully raised regional funding to meet March payments, indicating short-term liquidity management but unresolved structural issues

### 1.3 IMF Relations

- IMF suspended its program following the hidden debt disclosure (November 2025)
- Ongoing negotiations without new lending agreements as of late 2025
- Senegal seeking "reprofiling" (extending maturity) rather than formal restructuring, signaling preference for less painful debt relief mechanisms

**Policy Relevance:** Senegal's situation illustrates the gap between IMF requirements and sovereign government political constraints, the role of information asymmetries in debt crises, and real-time sovereign bond market responses to hidden debt disclosures.

## 4. BROADER WEST AFRICAN SOVEREIGN BOND LANDSCAPE

### 4.1 Regional Overview: WAEMU/UEMOA Framework

**Member States:** Benin, Burkina Faso, Côte d'Ivoire, Guinea-Bissau, Mali, Niger, Senegal, Togo

**Currency:** West African CFA Franc (XOF), issued by BCEAO

**Regional Exchange:** BRVM (Bourse Régionale des Valeurs Mobilières) - primary venue for domestic-currency debt

**Dual Listing Trend:** Increasing adoption of dual listings between BRVM and Luxembourg Stock Exchange (LGX), particularly for green/social bonds
- Example: CRRH-UEMOA's €91 million social bond (January 2026) listed on both BRVM and LGX

### 4.3 Listing Venue Patterns

**Observation:** West African sovereigns show clear preference hierarchy:

1. **Primary International Venue:** Luxembourg Stock Exchange (LuxSE) or Euronext Dublin
   - Rationale: EU/international investor access, regulatory familiarity
   - Côte d'Ivoire, Senegal, Benin primarily use these venues

2. **Regional Domestic Market:** BRVM (Bourse Régionale des Valeurs Mobilières)
   - For CFA franc-denominated debt
   - Growing dual-listing trend with LuxSE (2025-2026)

3. **Alternative International:** London Stock Exchange (Ghana notably present)
   - FCA listing represents alternative to EU venues
   - NSM filings for transparency

4. **Very Limited:** SEC EDGAR (US venues)
   - Senegal has SEC references but these appear to be historical/archived

## 8. POLICY & RESEARCH IMPLICATIONS

### 8.1 Information Gaps & Asymmetries

**Senegal Case Study Value:**
- Hidden debt scandal reveals prospectuses may not capture off-balance-sheet borrowing
- Prospectuses themselves are clean; the "missing" debt is the analytical gap
- Potential corpus research question: Can prospectus language predict hidden debt risk?

### 8.2 Market Venue Patterns

**Observation:** West African sovereigns split between:
- **Developed-market venues** (Luxembourg, Dublin, London) for international investor access
- **Regional venues** (BRVM) for local-currency debt and CFA franc investor bases
- Growing dual-listing trend suggests market maturation but also fragmentation

**Implication for Corpus:** Researchers tracking a single country's debt may need to query 2-3 venues simultaneously.

### 8.3 Regulatory Consistency

**EU Prospectus Standards:** Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire, and other West African sovereigns follow identical EU prospectus regulatory framework when listing on Euronext/LuxSE, enabling direct comparison of disclosure practices independent of home-country governance.
